YYC YYC Bus Audit · Project brief · One page

An open, reproducible model of Calgary’s bus network, built entirely from Open Calgary data

Calgary is rebalancing its bus network on August 31, arguing that express-route hours buy more as frequent core service. This project supplies the missing quantitative half of that argument: a public demand model and a constraint solver over bus frequencies, with every service guarantee asserted in code.

How it works

A Python pipeline reads Calgary’s published GTFS schedule and measures the supply side: vehicle-kilometres by route, period, and day type. A gravity demand model, built from the civic census, downtown cordon counts, and stop locations, is calibrated to Calgary Transit’s published monthly ridership totals. A constraint solver (CP-SAT) then searches over frequencies in 1,217 route-period-daytype cells, keeping only timetables that obey every service guarantee below, and reports the cheapest one. Everything is open source, and the results are a live, explorable map.

Service guarantees, asserted in code

  • The frequent network stays frequent: every route at 15 minutes or better keeps 15 minutes or better, Calgary’s own Primary Transit Network promise.
  • No rider waits more than double the original, anywhere.
  • Systemwide modeled ridership loss is hard-budgeted under 2%.
  • No stop loses service in any period it has today.
  • No route is shortened
The first, capacity-only optimizer “saved” 50% by gutting the BRT lines. That number was absurd, and the fix is the point: 50% → 35% → 10.4% as each guarantee was added. A savings estimate is only as strong as its constraint set.

Honest limits

  • Demand is modeled from public proxies, not measured. Calgary publishes no per-stop ridership, so results are ranges across three demand scenarios, not point claims.
  • The baseline is the July 2026 network. The August 31 service changes are a natural re-run, and the pipeline makes that a one-command update.
  • The realtime feeds (vehicle positions, trip updates) are being archived right now to capture a pre-change performance baseline for a follow-up analysis.

What would make it decision-grade

The model’s single biggest limitation is measured demand. Three additions to the open data portal would close most of the gap, and I will re-run and republish the full analysis, code and all, with any of them:

  • Stop-level or route-level boarding counts (the APC data Calgary Transit already collects), at any aggregation the city is comfortable publishing.
  • occupancy_status in the GTFS-Realtime vehicle positions feed: the spec supports it; the feed currently omits it.
  • Hourly downtown cordon counts: the current release is daily totals, which forces the model’s hourly shape onto canonical curves.
